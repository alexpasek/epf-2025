# EPF Blog Style Guide

## Voice

Write like a knowledgeable contractor explaining the job clearly to a homeowner.

The tone should be:

- Friendly
- Direct
- Practical
- Honest
- Confident
- Simple
- Helpful
- Not pushy
- Not corporate

## Writing style

Use clear contractor-style explanations.

Good style:

"In most homes, the issue is not just removing the texture. After popcorn ceiling removal, the ceiling usually still needs repair, skim coating, sanding, primer, and flat ceiling paint to look properly smooth."

Good style:

"Painted popcorn usually takes longer because water does not soften the texture the same way. That can change both the labour and the final refinishing needed."

Good style:

"A cheaper quote may only include scraping. Before comparing prices, make sure the quote includes refinishing, sanding, primer, paint, protection, and cleanup."

Avoid:

"Transform your living space into a breathtaking sanctuary."

Avoid:

"Our world-class experts revolutionize your home."

Avoid:

"Click here for unbeatable deals."

Avoid:

"Best cheap popcorn ceiling removal company near me."

## EPF positioning

Mention EPF strengths naturally, only when relevant:

- Careful floor and wall protection
- Dust-controlled sanding
- HEPA vacuum system
- Proper skim coating
- Smooth ceiling refinishing
- Level 5 finish when needed
- Flat ceiling paint
- Primer before paint
- Repair before painting
- Clean work process
- Realistic timeline
- Written quote after photos/site review

Do not overuse sales language.

## Price language

Use honest price explanations.

Always explain that price depends on:

- Painted vs unpainted popcorn
- Ceiling height
- Room size
- Total square footage
- Empty vs furnished home
- Wall/floor protection needs
- Repairs needed after removal
- Skylights
- Staircases
- Vaulted/high ceilings
- Primer and paint
- Cleanup
- Access and scheduling

Do not give one fixed price unless the user gave exact project details.

Use phrases like:

- "In many cases..."
- "A realistic range is..."
- "The final price depends on..."
- "For an accurate quote, photos and room sizes are needed..."

Avoid:

- "Always"
- "Guaranteed cheapest"
- "Perfect finish every time"
- "No dust at all"
- "Exact price without seeing the ceiling"

## Safety language

For asbestos or possible hazardous material:

- Do not give unsafe removal instructions.
- Recommend proper testing when age/risk is relevant.
- Avoid panic language.
- Be clear that safe handling matters.

For electrical work:

- Recommend licensed electrician where relevant.
- Do not tell homeowners to do unsafe wiring.

For water damage/mold:

- Recommend investigating the source before cosmetic repair.
- Avoid giving medical or hazardous-material claims.

## CTA style

Use natural CTAs.

Examples:

"For a proper quote, send a few wide photos, close-up photos, room sizes, ceiling height, and whether the popcorn has been painted."

"If you are comparing prices, make sure each quote includes removal, refinishing, sanding, primer, paint, protection, and cleanup."

"EPF Pro Services can review your photos and confirm the best approach before the project starts."

Avoid pushy CTAs.

Avoid:

- "Book now before it's too late!"
- "Don't miss this deal!"
- "We are the #1 best company!"

## Local style

For local blogs, write naturally.

Good:

"In Mississauga homes, pricing can change depending on whether the home is occupied, how much protection is needed, and whether the popcorn has been painted before."

Good:

"For Oakville projects with large windows and bright rooms, ceiling finishing quality matters because natural light can show imperfections."

Avoid fake local facts.

Do not mention specific neighbourhoods unless the project/page context actually supports it.

## Paragraph style

Use:

- Short paragraphs
- Clear H2/H3 sections
- Bullet lists when useful
- Simple explanations
- Direct answers

Avoid:

- Huge paragraphs
- Academic language
- Overly technical explanations
- Repeating the same point many times

## Blog page design style

All new EPF blog pages, across every service topic, should look like professional contractor field guides.

Match the design direction used on the newly created drywall patch guide, but adapt the content, images, field note, diagnostic section, local links, and CTAs to the specific service topic:

- Dark hero section with a strong image.
- Soft neutral page background.
- White or light neutral cards.
- Rounded cards with clean borders.
- Clear CTA buttons.
- Trust badges near CTAs.
- Early quick-answer card.
- Diagnostic tables or checklists near the top.
- Field note card for contractor insight related to the topic.
- Local service section when relevant.
- Related articles shown as cards.
- Author/trust block near the bottom.
- Strong bottom CTA.

Do not make any new EPF blog look like a plain text article.

Do not add design elements that make the page feel like a generic marketing landing page.

The page should feel useful first, then conversion-focused.

## EPF content examples

Use these ideas naturally:

- "Removal is only one part of the job."
- "The final smooth look comes from the refinishing stage."
- "Painted popcorn often changes the amount of labour."
- "Good ceiling paint should be flat, not shiny."
- "High natural light can reveal small imperfections."
- "A proper estimate should include preparation, protection, refinishing, primer, paint, and cleanup."
